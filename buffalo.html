Buffalo buffalo Buffalo buffalo buffalo buffalo Buffalo buffalo

// Broadstone Studios

'Zeitgeber': a rhythmically occurring natural phenomenon, acting as a cue for the regulation of circadian rhythms.

Translated from German, meaning "timer" or "time-giver", the zeitgeber presented itself at one point and another in Hannah Fitz's latest exhibition "Buffalo buffalo Buffalo buffalo buffalo buffalo Buffalo buffalo" at Broadstone Studios in Dublin. Curated by Lily Cahill & Rob Murphy, Fitz presented a series of sculptures that could be described in one dimension as a definitive collection of loop-like caricatures.

Exclusively sculptural, last week Broadstone Studios housed a series of garishly colourful, tacky and textured artworks sitting in its long-lived living-like room, under the imperious title of "Buffalo buffalo Buffalo buffalo buffalo buffalo Buffalo buffalo". The exhibition comprised of a sequence of free-standing sculptures, most of which prevailed at what seems to be an increasingly familiar 'one-somethingth' scale of an average human size. Made from craft-like materials such as paper, plastic, glue and wire, Fitz rendered sculptures mindfully imitating the appearances of domestic objects such as "Plant", "Bowl", and "Table". Even included were two figurine-like sculptures perched on the floor, all too similar to what might catch an eye on a stale mantlepiece. Each finished with a cheesy shade of gloss and shaped into a mock-up finish, the pieces on show resembled candid, cartoon versions of unspecified versions of themselves.

The entire exhibition coiled on itself. In a habitual turnstile, each piece prompted it's next iteration before taking the time to acknowledge its own self. Collectively, the works shared each others' material roots and brimmed contently with such astute site-specificity that it pitter-pattered brightly amidst the show's otherwise evasive demeanour. As the carpeted air of Broadstone Studios sat heavily on the work, the work too permeated the space. Each was breathing into the other, as if to attempt a synchronization of it's own circadian rhythm. This cadence seemed to generate an intrinsic hinge between the chunky artworks themselves, and the dead space hanging in the long room with the high ceilings. Consequently, the work at one point heaved and began to charge an innate cycle for the show, causing it to trip on itself again and again, as you try to familiarize yourself with the exhibition. It was a strange lapse, that made it tricky to view one single piece at a time.

As this cycle gained its momentum surrounding the plot of "Buffalo buffalo Buffalo buffalo buffalo buffalo Buffalo buffalo" small embellishments of measured slices became attentively conspicuous. The exhibition's recurring materials were comparatively placed on the carpet and on each other, as the works grasped for themselves and their surroundings in a resounding stupor.

The room turned to putty as the work induced a breed of veritable stickiness between the work's fabric and the room itself, crossing at a version of old and new (conceivably ruminating somewhere in the mass of carpet). Grabbed from behind by these property relatives, I wondered – for whose exemplification was this? It seems dubious to consider that a property might exist without an exemplifier, however if there was one present at this exhibition it was playing hide and seek on me.

The sculptures laid lingering in their constant double-take and would remain that way until the exemplifier (if any) of these properties would come to claim them. The works had been knotting themselves in systems and universals from the beginning, and every so often as "Buffalo buffalo Buffalo buffalo buffalo buffalo Buffalo buffalo" coincided with itself, the zeitgeber would appear to cue the rhythm.

As part of Lily Cahill and Rob Murphy's residency at Broadstone Studios the artists invited Hannah Fitz to exhibit her work from the 14th until the 18th of May, and previously Ruth Clinton and Niamh Moriarty to engage with the Victorian era building. This series of exhibitions finished with 'Prodigy' – a solo exhibition of work by Cahill & Murphy at Broadstone Studios, on May 28th.
